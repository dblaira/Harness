## ADAM

> Here is the structured list of the most widely quoted and applied Gestalt principles used in design today:

1. The Law of Prägnanz (Simplicity / Good Figure)
   What it is: The cornerstone of Gestalt; the mind organizes complex shapes into the most straightforward and most coherent version possible.
   Why it matters: It emphasizes that our brains prefer harmony and balance.
2. Proximity
   What it is: Objects placed close to each other are instinctively perceived as a group or related category.
   Why it matters: It is heavily used in typography and interface design to build clear visual hierarchies and sectioning.
3. Similarity
   What it is: The human eye groups elements that share visual characteristics (e.g., identical color, shape, or size) together, even when they are scattered.
   Why it matters: Allows quick identification of patterns, such as making sure all clickable links on a webpage share the same accent color.
4. Closure
   What it is: The brain automatically fills in the missing visual pieces of an incomplete shape or image to perceive the whole.
   Why it matters: Commonly utilized in clever logo design to convey maximum meaning with minimal visual information.
5. Continuity
   What it is: The eye naturally follows the smoothest path, line, or curve through an object.
   Why it matters: Guides the viewer's gaze effortlessly through a composition, ensuring they follow the flow of information.
6. Figure-Ground
   What it is: The brain instinctively isolates an object (the figure) from its broader surroundings (the background or ground).
   Why it matters: It plays with negative space to create striking visual double meanings (like the famous Rubin's vase optical illusion).
7. Common Fate
   What it is: Elements that move in the same direction or synchronize in unison are grouped together as a single entity.
   Why it matters: Essential for understanding animations, dropdown menus, or hovering states in user experience (UX) design.
8. Symmetry and Order
   What it is: We interpret symmetrical, balanced, and orderly shapes as grouped together and visually pleasing.
   Why it matters: Gives audiences a sense of stability, prompting them to understand an overall layout without exerting extra effort.
